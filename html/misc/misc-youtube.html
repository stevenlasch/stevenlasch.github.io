Critical Thinking

  • Introduction to critical thinking

What is Critical Thinking?

  • Making sure we have good reasons for our beliefs
  • Rational people want to have true beliefs
  • Form beliefs only when you have good reasons for them.

What is an argument?

  • A set of statements that together comprise a reason for a further statement
  • We call the arguments premises which lead to a conclusion
  • A good argument has premises that make the conclusion likely to be true
  • We need to evaluate arguments to determine if they are good or bad reasons

Two types of arguments

  1. Deductive: When the truth of the premises guarantees the truth of the conclusion
  2. Ampliative: The truth of the premises make the conclusion probable, but do NOt guarantee the truth of the conclusion
  3. Knowing the type of argument is essential to evaluate if the argument is good

How to remember everything you read

Reading Stages

  1. Consumption: Everything you consume must be digested.
  2. Digestive: This has to be balanced against the consumption.

Not all information is equal. What goes into your brain is less important than what stays in your brain

PACER Acronym

  1. Procedural: Any information on how something is executed. The key is practice.
  2. Analogous: When something is related to something you all ready know. The key is to critique how good the analogy is
  3. Conceptual: Facts and information. The key is mapping this information. Non-linear network like note taking.
  4. Evidence: Information to prove a conceptual point. The key is to store and rehearse this.
  5. Reference: The key is to store and rehearse this.
